17:T94c,Salmon quality varies dramatically. Professional evaluation prevents expensive mistakes.

**Visual Inspection Checklist:**

**Color Assessment (10 seconds)**
✓ Surface color: Should be bright orange-pink (not pale or gray)
✓ Uniformity: Is color consistent throughout the fillet?
✓ Sheen: Does it have a healthy, moist appearance?
✓ Discoloration: Any brown or gray areas (signs of aging)?

Color grading:
• Bright orange = AA-grade (fresh, high quality)
• Medium orange = A-grade (good, acceptable)
• Pale orange/gray = B-grade (aged, lower quality)

**Flesh Firmness (5 seconds)**
✓ Press with finger: Does it spring back immediately?
✓ Structure: Does the flesh hold its shape or appear soft?
✓ Separation: Are the muscle fibers tightly bound or separating?

Firmness grading:
• Firm, springs back immediately = AA-grade
• Moderately firm, slight delay = A-grade
• Soft, slow recovery = B-grade

**Aroma Test (5 seconds)**
✓ Fresh, ocean-like smell = Good quality
✓ Strong fishy odor = Borderline quality
✓ Ammonia or sour smell = REJECT

**Fat Content Assessment (Visual)**
✓ White marbling visible in muscle = Higher fat (18-22%)
✓ Minimal white streaks = Lower fat (15-17%)

Why it matters:
• Higher fat = Better flavor, more luxurious mouthfeel
• Norwegian salmon: Typically 18-22% fat
• Chilean salmon: Typically 16-19% fat
• Wild Alaskan: Typically 18-20% fat

**Age Assessment:**
Asking critical questions:
• "When was this harvested/processed?"
• "What's the cold-chain documentation?"
• "Temperature logs from processing to today?"

Acceptable age:
• AA-grade: <7 days old (ideally <5 days)
• A-grade: <10 days old
• B-grade: <14 days old
• Beyond 14 days: Only for cooking/processing applications

**Fileting Quality (If receiving whole fish):**
✓ Are fillets uniform thickness?
✓ Is the spine cleanly removed without excessive flesh loss?
✓ Are there any pin bones?
✓ Is the skin clean and undamaged?

**Taste Testing Protocol (If evaluating new supplier):**
1. Cook small sample (poach, 62°C for 6-8 minutes)
2. Taste fresh (before seasoning)
3. Evaluate: Flavor intensity, sweetness, texture, finish

19:Tf69,Salmon is highly perishable. Cold-chain management is critical to quality preservation.

**Cold-Chain Standards:**

**Temperature Requirements:**
✓ Optimal: -1°C to +2°C (ice-cold but not frozen)
✓ Acceptable: 0°C to +4°C
✗ Unacceptable: >4°C (bacterial growth risk)

**What happens at each temperature:**
• -1 to +2°C: Quality preserved, 7-10 day shelf life
• +2 to +4°C: Acceptable, 5-7 day shelf life
• +4 to +8°C: Marginal, 2-3 day shelf life (quality degradation)
• >+8°C: Dangerous, bacterial multiplication risk

**Cold-Chain Documentation:**
Every supplier should provide:
• Temperature logs: Time-stamped records from processing to delivery
• Ice/coolant specs: Type of coolant used (food-grade ice, gel packs, etc.)
• Insulation verification: Box insulation thickness and integrity
• Handling protocols: Notes on any temperature excursions

**Delivery Logistics:**

**Optimal delivery protocol:**
• Scheduled delivery time: Same day/time weekly (e.g., "Tuesday 10am")
• Advance notice: 3-5 days to allow restaurant planning
• Insulated packaging: Foam box with adequate ice/coolant
• Temperature verification: Supplier should include temperature probe reading
• Unloading procedure: Inspect temperature; if >4°C, refuse delivery

**Receiving protocol:**
✓ Check delivery temperature immediately
✓ Verify weight (against invoice)
✓ Visual inspection for damage
✓ Smell test (if any off-odor, refuse)
✓ Check expiration/"best by" date
✓ Document receipt time
✓ Place in cold storage immediately

**Storage Management:**

**Dedicated salmon storage:**
• Temperature: -1°C to +2°C (separate from other proteins)
• Duration: Maximize 7 days maximum (better if used within 5 days)
• Organization: FIFO (first in, first out)
• Rotation: Check inventory daily, use oldest stock first
• Packaging: Keep original packaging until use (protects from oxidation)

**Freezing (if necessary):**
• Can extend shelf life to 2-3 months at -18°C
• Use: Lower quality applications (cooking, processing)
• Quality loss: Thawing causes 10-15% moisture loss and texture change
• Thawing: Always use refrigeration method (24-48 hours at +2°C)
• Never: Thaw at room temperature (bacterial risk)

**Inventory Management:**

**Stock levels by volume:**
• High-volume restaurant (100+ salmon dishes/month):
  - Storage capacity needed: ~50-70 kg at any time
  - Order frequency: Weekly (fresh inventory cycle)
  - Safety stock: 2 weeks supply (in case of disruption)

• Medium-volume restaurant (30-50 salmon dishes/month):
  - Storage capacity needed: ~20-30 kg
  - Order frequency: Bi-weekly
  - Safety stock: 1-2 weeks

**Managing waste:**
• Fresh salmon utilization: 85-90% (skin/bones are trimmings)
• Trim applications: Stock/sauce making (bone broth, dashi)
• Spoilage rate: <5% for well-managed operations
• Cost implications: 5% spoilage = HKD 1,500-2,000/month in waste (for 30kg/month buyer)

**Troubleshooting Cold-Chain Issues:**

**Problem: Salmon arrived at +6°C**
Cause: Delivery delay, insufficient ice, poor insulation
Action: REFUSE delivery or heavily discount (quality compromised)
Future prevention: 
• Specify cooler requirements in contract
• Request temperature guarantee
• Consider alternative suppliers if pattern repeats

**Problem: Fish has been in storage 9 days**
Cause: Over-ordering or slower usage than planned
Action: Use for cooking only (sauces, tartare, ceviche)
Future prevention:
• Improve ordering accuracy
• Coordinate with chef on usage projections
• Consider more frequent smaller deliveries

**Problem: Melted ice in delivery box**
Cause: Inadequate insulation or extended delivery time

• Responsible farm management
• Environmental protection (no habitat damage)
• Fish welfare standards (feeding, stocking density, health)
• Social responsibility (worker conditions)
• Supply chain traceability

Farmed salmon sources with ASC:
• Norwegian farms: 60-70% ASC-certified
• Chilean farms: 50-60% ASC-certified
• Scottish farms: 60-70% ASC-certified
• Pricing impact: Minimal (<5% premium)

Why corporations require it:
• Michelin Guide: Now considering sustainability in evaluation
• Luxury hotels: Corporate ESG commitments require certified suppliers
• Insurance/compliance: Some policies require supplier certifications
• Brand story: Increasingly marketing 'responsibly farmed' salmon

**MSC (Marine Stewardship Council) Certification:**

Applies to wild-caught salmon:
• Alaskan wild salmon: 80%+ carries MSC certification
• Canadian wild salmon: 60-70% MSC-certified
• Norwegian wild salmon: <20% (limited wild fisheries)

What it verifies:
• Fishery is not overfished
• Fishing practices minimize environmental impact
• Fishery is well-managed and regulated

Business value:
• Marketing advantage: "MSC-certified wild salmon"
• Corporate requirement: Many Michelin restaurants require this
• Price support: Can justify 5-10% premium for certified wild

• "Norwegian farmed salmon, ASC-certified"
