1a:T8da,Japan's grading system is the global standard for uni. It reflects how Japanese producers evaluated urchin for centuries and remains the most widely recognized classification.

**AA Grade (最高級 - Saikokyu)**
Definition: Sushi-bar only. The finest 5-10% of each harvest.
• Color: Uniformly bright golden-orange (#FFD700 in hex), no grayness or dullness
• Size & Shape: Small, plump lobes (typically 1.5-2.5cm) with perfect symmetry
• Texture: Firm yet creamy, holds precise shape, collapses slightly on tongue
• Aroma: Sweet ocean aroma, zero ammonia, zero fishy notes
• Liquid: Minimal (<5% of lobe weight), completely clear
• Origin: Hokkaido Bafun (peak season June-August) or premium Canadian Red (limited)
• Certification: Harvest date within 48 hours, cold-chain documentation
• Cost: $120-180 per kg wholesale (Hong Kong pricing)

**A Grade (優級 - Yuukyu)**
Definition: Premium restaurant use. 15-25% of harvest.
• Color: Golden-orange with acceptable variation, no green or brown hues
• Size & Shape: Medium lobes (2-3cm), good symmetry, some minor variation
• Texture: Firm, holds shape well, minor texture variation acceptable
• Aroma: Sweet, clean ocean aroma, trace ammonia only, no off-flavors
• Liquid: Moderate (5-10%), clear color
• Origin: Japanese sources (Murasaki, minor Bafun), Canadian Red
• Certification: Harvest date within 4-5 days, standard cold-chain
• Cost: $70-100 per kg wholesale

**B Grade (普級 - Fukyuu)**
Definition: Culinary applications, not raw service. 25-40% of harvest.
• Color: Orange with acceptable discoloration, may show minor browning
• Size & Shape: Larger lobes (3-4cm), irregular shape acceptable
• Texture: Softer, less structural integrity, slight mushiness acceptable
• Aroma: Slight ammonia scent acceptable, minor off-flavors tolerable
• Liquid: High (10-20%), may be slightly cloudy
• Origin: Lower-grade Japanese, older harvests, processing urchin
• Cost: $25-50 per kg wholesale

**C Grade (加工級 - Kakou-kyu)**
Definition: Processing only (paste, sauce). Not used for whole-lobe service.

1b:T560,Canadian red urchin (from British Columbia) has become a legitimate AA-grade alternative. Understanding how it compares to Japanese varieties is essential for modern sourcing.

**Canadian Red vs. Hokkaido Bafun:**

Bafun (Japanese standard for comparison):
• Size: Small (1.5-2.5 cm), delicate appearance
• Flavor: Rich, intensely sweet, umami-forward
• Texture: Extremely creamy, delicate
• Seasonality: 2-3 months peak (June-August)
• Supply: Constrained, premium pricing

Canadian Red:
• Size: Larger (2.5-3.5 cm), more robust appearance
• Flavor: Sweet and buttery, less intense than Bafun, cleaner finish
• Texture: Firmer than Bafun, stands up better to plating
• Seasonality: 6-8 months peak (October-May) — fills the Hokkaido gap perfectly
• Supply: More abundant, 25-35% cost advantage vs. Bafun

1c:T7d5,Before accepting any uni delivery, conduct this rapid visual inspection. Takes 30 seconds per tray.

**Step 1: Color Assessment (10 seconds)**
✓ Check the brightest lobe: Is it vibrant golden-orange?
✓ Check for uniformity: Are all lobes similar color?
✓ Look for discoloration: Any gray, green, or brown hues?
✓ Grade determination:
  - Uniform bright orange = likely AA
  - Mostly orange, some variation = likely A
  - Dull orange, gray patches = likely B

**Step 2: Texture & Firmness (10 seconds)**
✓ Observe lobe structure: Do they hold their shape or appear collapsed?
✓ Look for liquid: How much liquid surrounds the lobes?
✓ Check for mushiness: Any lobes that appear mushy or wet?
✓ Grade determination:
  - Firm, minimal liquid, perfect shape = AA/A
  - Moderately firm, some liquid = A
  - Soft, significant liquid = B

**Step 3: Aroma Test (5 seconds)**
✓ Open container and smell immediately
✓ Is it sweet ocean aroma? (Good)
✓ Do you detect ammonia? (Bad — reject)
✓ Any fishy or sour notes? (Bad — reject)
✓ Aroma assessment:
  - Sweet, clean = AA/A grade acceptable
  - Slight ammonia = B grade only
  - Strong ammonia or sour = REJECT

**Step 4: Certification Verification (30 seconds)**
✓ Check harvest date: Should be within 48 hours for AA, 4-5 days for A
✓ Verify producer ID: Hokkaido traceability number or Canadian license
✓ Review cold-chain documentation: Temperature logs should show 2-4°C throughout
✓ Check grading certificate: If provided, should match your assessment

**Rejection Criteria — ALWAYS Reject:**
• Ammonia aroma (safety risk)
• Excessive liquid (indicator of age/spoilage)
• Collapsed, mushy lobes (quality failure)
• Harvest date >7 days old
• Missing certification or cold-chain documentation
• Color that doesn't match stated grade

20:T5cf,**Mistake #1: Confusing "Premium" with "AA-Grade"**
Any supplier can call product "premium." Insist on specific grade (AA, A, or B) and reference the Japanese grading standard. If they can't articulate grade criteria, they don't know what they're selling.

**Mistake #2: Accepting Age as an Excuse**
Don't accept: "This is 5-day-old uni, but it's still good quality." Age affects flavor and safety. AA-grade should be <48 hours. A-grade <4-5 days. Full stop.

**Mistake #3: Ignoring Aroma**
Ammonia aroma is not a minor quality flaw — it's a food safety indicator. Ammonia means bacteria are multiplying. ALWAYS reject. No exceptions.

**Mistake #4: Not Tasting Before Service**
Visual inspection catches most issues, but taste testing catches off-flavors that indicate spoilage or poor handling. Taste one lobe from each delivery. Takes 10 seconds.

**Mistake #5: Not Requesting Documentation**
Traceability documentation is now standard. Any supplier who can't provide harvest date, cold-chain logs, and origin certification is cutting corners. Don't work with them.

**Mistake #6: Confusing Origin with Quality**
"Hokkaido" doesn't automatically mean AA-grade. Canadian Red at AA-grade can be superior to lower-grade Japanese uni. Evaluate by grade, not by marketing.

**Mistake #7: Accepting Inconsistent Supply**
If your supplier sometimes sends AA and sometimes sends A without clear communication, that's a supplier problem.
