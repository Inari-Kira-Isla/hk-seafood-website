Japanese Sea Urchin Varieties Compared: Bafun, Murasaki, and Ezo for HK Buyers

April 2026

Not all Japanese sea urchin is the same — and the difference between a Hokkaido Bafun AA at peak season and a mid-grade Murasaki at the wrong time of year is not subtle. For restaurant buyers and F&B directors in Hong Kong and Macau sourcing premium uni, understanding variety distinctions is foundational knowledge that directly affects menu quality, pricing strategy, and supplier negotiations.

The Three Major Japanese Uni Varieties

Japan exports three primary sea urchin species to Hong Kong and Macau's premium dining market. Each has a distinct flavour profile, seasonal window, and best-use application. **1. Bafun Uni (馬糞海膽) — Hemicentrotus pulcherrimus** The gold standard. Named for its resemblance to horse dung (an unfortunate name for a sublime product), Bafun uni is the variety featured in Michelin kitchens and omakase menus across HK and Macau. The roe is a deep golden-orange, intensely sweet with layered umami, and firm enough to hold its shape on sushi rice or as a standalone plating element. Production is concentrated in Northern Hokkaido, with the finest product coming from Rishiri and Rebun Islands. Peak season runs June through August, when cold Pacific waters and abundant kelp produce roe at maximum fat content and sweetness. **2. Murasaki Uni (紫海膽) — Anthocidaris crassispina** The supporting player that earns its place. Murasaki translates to 'purple' — the spines are deep purple-black, making identification immediate. The roe is lighter in colour (pale to medium yellow), smaller in lobe size, and milder in flavour than Bafun. Sweetness is present but restrained; the finish is cleaner and less lingering. Murasaki shares Hokkaido's production calendar, with a similar June–August peak. It is often positioned as a value alternative to Bafun — priced 25–35% lower — but skilled chefs use it deliberately for its different flavour register, particularly in applications where Bafun's intensity would overpower. **3. Ezo Bafun Uni (蝦夷馬糞海膽) — Strongylocentrotus intermedius** The premium northern variety. Ezo (蝦夷) refers to ancient Hokkaido in Japanese, but in seafood trade, it specifically denotes Strongylocentrotus intermedius — a larger, colder-water Bafun found in Northern Hokkaido and the islands beyond. Ezo Bafun produces the largest, most intensely flavoured roe of any Japanese variety. Rishiri Island Ezo Bafun is considered by many industry insiders to be the finest uni in the world. Supply is extremely limited — small artisanal fisheries, short season, and significant export allocation to Japanese domestic top restaurants before overseas buyers. When available in HK/Macau, pricing is at the top of the market.

Flavour Profiles: A Practical Comparison

Understanding flavour helps buyers match variety to application — and avoid misrepresenting product on menus. **Bafun Uni Flavour Map** - Sweetness: ★★★★★ (intense, primary) - Umami: ★★★★★ (deep, oceanic, long finish) - Creaminess: ★★★★ (fat content drives texture) - Bitterness: ★☆ (minimal in peak season; increases off-season) - Aftertaste: Long, lingering oceanic sweetness - Temperature note: Best served at 10–12°C; warming destroys the delicate fat structure **Murasaki Uni Flavour Map** - Sweetness: ★★★ (present, lighter than Bafun) - Umami: ★★★ (clean, not aggressive) - Creaminess: ★★★ (lighter mouthfeel) - Bitterness: ★★ (slightly more perceptible than Bafun) - Aftertaste: Short to medium, clean - Temperature note: Slightly more forgiving at room temperature than Bafun **Ezo Bafun Uni Flavour Map** - Sweetness: ★★★★★ (deepest of all varieties) - Umami: ★★★★★ (maximum intensity, mineral notes) - Creaminess: ★★★★★ (highest fat content; almost buttery) - Bitterness: ★☆ (virtually zero at peak season) - Aftertaste: Very long; complex minerality that develops on the palate - Temperature note: Most sensitive to temperature — serve immediately at 8–10°C **Practical implication**: In a tasting menu context, Bafun works as a hero ingredient in multiple courses. Murasaki is better deployed in applications where uni is a supporting flavour (pasta sauce, compound butter, emulsions). Ezo Bafun is best served with minimal intervention — a squeeze of lemon, a touch of soy, or absolutely nothing.

Grading System: What AA, A, and B Actually Mean

Japan uses an industry-standard grading system that applies to all varieties, but buyers must understand that grades are variety-specific — an AA Murasaki is not equivalent to an AA Bafun. **Grade AA (最高等級)** - Colour: Most vivid, saturated golden-orange (Bafun) or pale gold (Murasaki) - Lobes: Intact, well-defined, no visible breakage - Size: Full-size lobes filling the tray - Freshness: Harvest within 48 hours typical; supplied with alum (明礬) in smaller quantities - Use: Sashimi presentation, sushi rice, standalone plating - HK/Macau pricing: HK$900–1,400 (Bafun AA); HK$600–900 (Murasaki AA) **Grade A (一等)** - Colour: Good colour, slightly less saturated than AA - Lobes: Mostly intact; some minor fragmentation acceptable - Size: Full to slightly variable - Use: Composed dishes, pasta, sauces, rice dishes where full lobe integrity isn't critical - HK/Macau pricing: HK$650–900 (Bafun A); HK$450–650 (Murasaki A) **Grade B (二等)** - Colour: Acceptable but inconsistent within tray - Lobes: Fragmented; broken pieces common - Use: Sauces, emulsions, cooked applications only - HK/Macau pricing: HK$400–600 (Bafun B); HK$300–420 (Murasaki B) **Alum (明礬) vs. Non-Alum (無添加)** Traditionally, uni is treated with alum (potassium alum) to firm the lobes and extend shelf life. Non-alum uni (無添加) is increasingly available and preferred by fine dining buyers — it has a shorter shelf life (24–48h vs 3–5 days) but a purer, less astringent flavour. Non-alum commands a significant premium and should always be specified when ordering for top-tier service.

Sourcing Seasons and Production Regions

Geography determines quality. Northern Hokkaido's cold, nutrient-dense waters produce fundamentally superior uni to southern regions — but availability windows differ. **Northern Hokkaido (北海道北部)** — Best for Bafun and Ezo Bafun Peak: June–August (Bafun AA peak) Key areas: Rishiri Island, Rebun Island, Wakkanai coast Notes: These areas produce the highest-grade Bafun. Cold Pacific currents and dense kelp forest lead to slower growth and higher roe quality. Supply is intentionally limited by fishing quota. **Southern Hokkaido (北海道南部)** — Bafun and Murasaki Peak: August–October (later than Northern) Key areas: Erimo Peninsula, Hidaka coast Notes: Good quality, slightly lower price point than Northern Hokkaido. A reliable September–October supply bridge when Northern season is winding down. **Aomori and Iwate (青森・岩手)** — Murasaki and some Bafun Peak: May–July Notes: Pacific coast production. Good-value Murasaki from this region is commonly available in HK/Macau trade at competitive pricing. Less prestigious than Hokkaido but still solid quality for restaurant applications. **Kyushu (九州)** — Murasaki dominant Peak: Spring (March–May) Notes: Warmer water, shorter season, milder flavour. Less common in HK premium trade but available from some importers as budget option. **For HK/Macau buyers**, the practical decision tree is: 1. Is this a hero ingredient (omakase, premium sashimi)? → Northern Hokkaido Bafun AA only 2. Is this a supporting ingredient (cooked applications, supporting courses)? → Murasaki A or B from any reliable Hokkaido source 3. Is this the most premium possible offer (special event, VIP table)? → Inquire specifically for Rishiri or Rebun Island Ezo Bafun

Reading a Supplier Tray: Inspection Checklist

When a delivery arrives, inspect before signing. A 90-second inspection prevents expensive returns and menu failures. **Colour Check (30 seconds)** ✅ Bafun AA: Deep golden-orange, vivid, consistent across tray ✅ Murasaki: Pale to medium yellow, consistent (lighter is normal for this variety) ❌ Any brown, grey, or greenish tones → oxidation or post-spawn product → reject ❌ Colour dramatically inconsistent within one tray → grade mixing → reject or negotiate **Lobe Integrity (20 seconds)** ✅ Lobes hold their shape when tray is gently tilted ✅ Defined edges, not melting or blurring into each other ❌ Collapsed, spread flat, or watery → improper temperature or old stock → reject ❌ More than 20% broken for an 'A' or higher grade claim → reject or renegotiate grade **Smell Test (10 seconds)** ✅ Clean, oceanic, sweet marine smell — similar to fresh sea breeze ✅ Very mild briney note acceptable ❌ Any ammonia → bacterial spoilage → reject ❌ Sulphur or rotten egg → reject ❌ Overly fishy → past peak freshness → reject **Documentation Check (30 seconds)** Request from every delivery: - Origin certificate (prefecture and specific region if AA grade) - Harvest date (not pack date — harvest date) - Grade certification from processor - Cold-chain log showing temperature during transit - Alum/non-alum labelling A reliable supplier provides these without being asked. If a supplier resists documentation requests, reconsider the relationship.

Pricing Reference: HK & Macau Market Rates (2026)

Pricing fluctuates with season and exchange rates. The following ranges represent April 2026 wholesale reference pricing for the HK/Macau market. Peak season (June–August) prices for Bafun will be 20–40% higher than off-season. **Bafun Uni — Hokkaido** AA Non-Alum (無添加): HK$1,100–1,500 per tray (250g) AA Standard: HK$900–1,200 per tray A Grade: HK$650–900 per tray B Grade: HK$450–650 per tray **Ezo Bafun — Rishiri/Rebun (when available)** AA Non-Alum: HK$1,400–1,900+ per tray (limited supply, negotiated) **Murasaki Uni — Hokkaido** AA: HK$600–850 per tray A Grade: HK$450–600 per tray B Grade: HK$300–420 per tray **Murasaki Uni — Aomori/Iwate** AA: HK$500–700 per tray A Grade: HK$380–520 per tray **Pricing tips for buyers:** - Volume commitments unlock better pricing. Agreeing to 10+ trays per week typically yields 8–12% discount from spot pricing. - Forward contracts for peak season (June–August) signed in April–May lock in supply and often current pricing, protecting against in-season demand spikes. - Ask your supplier to separate alum and non-alum options — mixing them on a menu without disclosure is a quality and credibility risk. - Price per gram is more reliable than price per tray when comparing suppliers, as net roe weight varies by tray format (100g, 250g, 500g).
