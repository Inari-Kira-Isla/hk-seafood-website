• Certification "pending" = they don't have it yet

Questions to ask:
• What's your typical lead time for orders?
• How often can you miss an order deadline?
• What's your backup plan if normal supply is disrupted?

• Your menu planning depends on consistent supply

• Seasonal surprises cause menu chaos

• How do you grade your products?
• What's your quality control process?
• Can you guarantee grade consistency?

• "Quality" is subjective; specific grading prevents surprises
• Consistent grading enables consistent menu pricing
• Quality guarantees protect your reputation
• If they can't guarantee quality, they're unreliable

Evaluation process:
• Request samples of each grade they offer
• Taste-test and compare against your quality standards
• Ask about their internal QC process
• Evaluate how they handle quality complaints

• What's your current pricing for [specific product/grade]?
• What volume commitments reduce pricing?
• How often do you adjust prices?
• What's your pricing structure during peak season?

• Pricing is typically 20-30% of food COGS
• Small per-kg savings compound to significant margins
• Volume leverage can reduce costs 10-20%
• Predictable pricing enables accurate menu margins

Evaluation process:
• Get 3 competing quotes for your typical monthly order
• Calculate true cost (including delivery, handling, waste)
• Ask about volume tiers and long-term discount programs
• Don't just pick the cheapest — evaluate price vs. quality trade-off

17:Tbc9,Most restaurants accept whatever terms suppliers offer. Strategic operators negotiate.

**Key Contract Elements to Negotiate:**

**Pricing Structure**
```
Don't accept: "Market price, we'll let you know"
Instead negotiate: "HKD 800/kg for 10-20kg/month, HKD 750/kg for 20+kg/month"

Good structure:
- Base price: Clearly stated per kg
- Volume tiers: Price reduction at 20kg, 50kg, etc.
- Price review frequency: Quarterly, not monthly
- Seasonal adjustment: ±5% max, defined by month
- Price lock period: Minimum 6 months
```

**Delivery Terms**
```
Don't accept: "We'll deliver sometime this week"
Instead negotiate: "Tuesday 10am, ±1 hour window, HKD X delivery fee included"

Good structure:
- Delivery day & time: Specific, predictable schedule
- Consistency guarantee: 95%+ on-time performance
- Failure remedies: Late delivery = 5% discount, miss 2x/quarter = contract review
- Location: Clearly specified (back door, loading dock, etc.)
- Temperature: Cold chain maintained, temperature logs provided
```

**Quality Guarantees**
```
Don't accept: "We sell quality product"
Instead negotiate: "AA-grade per Japanese standard, visual inspection & taste test verification"

Good structure:
- Grade specification: Reference specific international standard
- Testing protocol: How quality is verified
- Replacement guarantee: Timeline for rejection and replacement
- Failure threshold: 2 rejections per quarter = contract review
- Aroma & texture standards: Written specification of acceptable vs. reject criteria
```

**Minimum Order & Flexibility**
```
Don't accept: "Minimum 20kg, all-or-nothing"
Instead negotiate: "Minimum 10kg, ±20% weekly variation without penalty"

Good structure:
- Minimum order: Set at operationally feasible level (10-15kg typical)
- Order notice: 3 business days advance notice required
- Flexibility: Up to ±20% week-to-week variation without surcharge
- Seasonal adjustment: 2x/year review of minimums (seasonal peaks)
- Cancellation: Can cancel standing order with 2 weeks notice
```

**Communication & Reporting**
```
Don't accept: "We'll reach out if something's wrong"
Instead negotiate: "Weekly availability update every Friday 5pm, 24-hour response to inquiries"

Good structure:
- Weekly harvest report: What's available, what's coming, any supply issues
- Incident response: 24-hour reply to quality/delivery issues
- Contact protocol: Direct phone/email to account manager
- Pre-season planning: March (salmon) and May (sea urchin) consultation calls
- Quarterly review: Scheduled check-in to assess partnership health
```

**Payment Terms**
```
Don't accept: "Payment upfront"
Instead negotiate: "Net 30 (payment due 30 days after invoice)"

Good structure:
- Standard: Net 30 days is market standard for restaurants
- Early payment discount: 2% discount if paid within 10 days (optional)
- Invoice timing: Invoice with delivery, not after
- Payment method: ACH/bank transfer standard (no cash)
- Disputes: 5-day window to raise quality concerns; payment still due

**The First 90 Days: Establish Trust**

**Week 1-2: Onboarding**
- Introduce supplier contact to your kitchen team
- Explain your quality standards and why they matter
- Schedule first delivery and conduct joint quality verification
- Document baseline: taste-test their product, set quality reference standard
- Action: Send welcome email with contract, key contacts, emergency procedures

**Week 3-8: Consistent Ordering**
- Place regular orders on agreed schedule
- Provide feedback (positive and constructive) after each delivery
- Test different product variations if available
- Build routine: Tuesday 10am becomes predictable
- Action: Send brief weekly feedback message (3 sentences: "Great quality Tuesday, flavor excellent, temperature logs perfect")

**Week 9-12: First Strategic Conversation**
- Call supplier and discuss seasonal planning
- Share 6-month menu projection (if available)
- Discuss upcoming peaks (peak season buying, known events, etc.)
- Negotiate any pricing adjustments based on volume
- Action: Formal quarterly review call, document outcomes

**Beyond 90 Days: Partnership Deepening**

**Quarterly Communication Cadence:**

Quarter 1 (January-March):
- Review prior quarter performance (on-time %, quality rejections, pricing)
- Plan Q2 ordering and seasonal availability
- Discuss any operational changes affecting demand
- Action: Scheduled 30-minute call, written summary

Quarter 2 (April-June):
- Pre-season consultation: salmon peak coming
- Discuss market trends and upcoming supply challenges
- Lock in Q3 pricing
- Action: 30-minute call, update contract if needed

Quarter 3 (July-September):
- Evaluate relationship: staying or adjusting?
- Discuss next-year planning and growth
- Share customer feedback about product
- Action: 30-minute call, renewal discussion

Quarter 4 (October-December):
- Seasonal peak management: coordinate delivery schedules
- Plan contract renewal terms
- Discuss next-year projections
- Action: 30-minute call, finalize renewal

**Deepening Trust Through Transparency:**

What to share with suppliers:
- Your menu changes (helps them anticipate demand)
- Guest feedback about their product (they care about quality)
- Your business challenges (they may offer solutions)
- Upcoming events or peaks (so they can plan supply)
- Success stories (when their product helps your business)

What NOT to share:
- Your pricing to other suppliers
- Detailed financial information
- Complaints about delivery to third parties
- Plans to switch suppliers (until you've decided)

**When to Escalate Issues:**

Small issue (isolated quality problem, one late delivery):
- Handle with regular contact person
- Solution: replacement product, minor discount
- Follow-up: brief message confirming resolution

Moderate issue (repeat quality problem, pattern of lateness):
- Escalate to supplier management
- Request meeting to discuss root causes
- Solution: revised procedures, pricing adjustment, or partnership review
- Follow-up: written summary of discussion and action items

Severe issue (missed multiple orders, significant quality failures, certification lapse):
- Formal written notice of contract breach
- 10-day cure period: "Fix this or relationship ends"
- Parallel action: Identify backup supplier
- Decision point: Renegotiate or terminate

**Red Flags: When to Start Looking for New Suppliers**
- Missed deliveries more than 1x per quarter
- Quality rejections exceed agreed threshold
- Response time exceeds 24 hours regularly
- Certification lapses without notification
- Prices increase >10% outside agreed adjustment schedule
- Delivery temperatures out of specification

If you see 2+ red flags, start evaluating alternatives. **Portfolio Approach:**

**Primary Supplier (70% of volume)**
- Best relationship, most consistent quality
- Best pricing due to volume
- Usually provides 70% of monthly needs
- Your "go-to" partner

**Secondary Supplier (20% of volume)**
- Backup for supply disruptions
- Alternative quality/style (e.g., Norwegian vs. Canadian sea urchin)
- Maintains competitive pressure on primary supplier
- Used during supply constraints

**Tertiary Supplier (10% of volume)**
- Emergency backup only
- Tests new options/emerging suppliers
- Safety net if both primary and secondary fail

**Why This Works:**

**Risk Management:**
- If primary supplier has supply issues, secondary steps in
- Diversifies origin (reduces geographic risk)
- Protects against price manipulation

**Quality Leverage:**
- Primary supplier knows you have alternatives
- Encourages competitive quality and pricing
- Prevents complacency

**Menu Flexibility:**
- Can offer seasonal variety (Canadian + Norwegian sea urchin)
- Can compare quality and flavors
- Discover backup if primary quality dips

**Cost Management:**
- 70/20/10 split: Leverage volume discounts at primary
- Maintain secondary at competitive but not lowest pricing
- Avoid being dependent on any single supplier

- Negotiate premium pricing (suppliers charge more for peak supply)
- Lock in delivery schedule
- Arrange extra storage/handling if needed

**June-August (Summer Peak)**
Why: Summer entertaining, overseas tourists, graduation events
Typical peak: 40-50% higher volume
Action: April-May communication
- "We expect 70kg sea urchin peak June-August"
- Negotiate allocation if global supply is tight
- Hokkaido bafun peaks in June-July — order now
- Canadian Red becomes less available (not peak)
- Plan menu around available supply, not vice versa

**September-October (Shoulder Season)**
Why: Business entertaining, new season menus
Typical peak: 10-20% above baseline
Action: July-August communication
- Transition from summer to fall supply
- Price changes (Hokkaido ends, Canadian Red begins)
- Update inventory and ordering

**November-December (Holiday Peak)**
Why: Christmas, New Year, corporate entertaining
Typical peak: 35-45% above baseline
Action: September-October communication
- Holiday menu planning requires early communication
- Christmas demand starts mid-November
- New Year's entertaining peaks December 27-31
- Lock in delivery schedule to avoid shortages

**Key Negotiation Points for Peak Supply:**

**Allocation: "Can You Guarantee Supply?"**
Don't accept: "We'll try to get it"
Instead negotiate: "Guarantee 50kg in June at HKD 800/kg, with 95% fulfillment"

Good contract language:
```
"During peak periods (June 1-August 31), supplier commits to fulfilling
95% of ordered volume at contracted pricing. If unable to fulfill, supplier
will notify buyer by [date] with alternative options and pricing."
```

**Pricing: "What's the Premium?"**
Don't accept: "Prices will be higher during peak"
Instead negotiate: "Base HKD 800/kg plus HKD 50/kg peak premium June 1-July 31"

Good contract language:
```
"Peak pricing surcharge (June 1-July 31): +HKD 50/kg. This premium applies
to volume above 20kg/month baseline. Surcharge announced 30 days in advance.
No surcharge for volume below baseline."
```

**Storage & Handling: "What's Your Capacity?"**
Don't accept: "We'll deliver it all at once"
Instead negotiate: "Split shipments: Tuesday 10am and Friday 4pm to accommodate storage capacity"

Consider:
- How much freezer/cold storage do you have?
- Can you receive split shipments to spread cold-chain stress?
- Do you need special handling for peak volume?
- Are there staff capacity constraints?

**Advance Payment: "Can You Order Early?"**
Most peak supply is harvested months before peak demand. Good suppliers know this.

Negotiate:
- "Can we place June orders by March 1 with partial advance payment?"
- "What's your advance order discount (typically 2-5% discount for early commitment)"
- "What's your cancellation policy if our needs change?"

Example:
```
May 1: Restaurant communicates expected June-July peak (70kg sea urchin)
June 1: Supplier secures Hokkaido fishery allocation and confirms pricing
July 1: Final quantities confirmed; restaurant pays 50% deposit
August 1: Delivery begins; balance due on standard Net 30 terms
